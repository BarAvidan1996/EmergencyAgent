Why is the Siren So Loud?

It’s natural for children to be afraid of sirens and ask if they can be turned down or replaced with a more pleasant sound. Here’s how to mediate the topic for them

Many children are afraid of sirens, sometimes even more than of the missiles themselves. This fear - that the siren could occur suddenly and interrupt whatever they’re doing at the time - causes high physical tension. As a result, children may remain tense even between sirens, and can actually do nothing else but wait for the next siren.

Furthermore, many children ask “Can = the sound of the siren = be replaced by something nicer, like = an ice cream truck tune?” Others ask “Why can’t we just make the siren quieter?”

Speaking with children about topics they find threatening can give them a sense of security and competency to cope. Mediating reality enables connecting to children's inner world and provides an opportunity to create order, organize their experiences and process their emotions. An adult’s job is to help children understand what threats they face and how they can cope, in the emotional and behavioral dimensions, in a manner suitable to developmental stage.

When it comes to talking with children about the siren, it’s very important to explain its purpose - to protect us. Without the siren, we wouldn’t know that a missile has been fired in our direction and that we immediately need to enter the secure space for protection.

When discussing the siren with children, tell them that the siren is so loud because its job is to arouse us into action, to quit whatever it is we were doing and head promptly to the protected space. If the sirens would be quiet or have a pleasant tune, we wouldn’t be sufficiently alerted into action.

In such a conversation, we recommend talking with young children about different sounds and their meaning. You can do this through a game, by asking riddles about various sounds, such as the school bell, the sirens of police cars, fire trucks, and ambulances, the siren played on Memorial Day (Yom Hazikaron) and Holocaust Remembrance Day (Yom Hashoa), the siren that warns of missiles, and others.

Q&A

What to do if children react in an extreme way to emergencies?

How a child responds to a traumatic event depends on their age, personality, severity and proximity to the traumatic event and the level of support he receives from their family and friends. A range of different responses are normal in emergencies, and most children will recover from a crisis without professional help, with the help of those close to them. However, when the symptoms are extreme and last for more than a month after the emergency, professional help should be sought.

How should the family conversation be conducted when there are significant age differences between the children at home?

The conversation between the older children and the younger children can be split, to allow everyone to express themselves according to their needs and abilities. In a family with older children, it is better to have the conversation at the beginning of the preparation process and share with them the considerations in the division of tasks, the choice of the protected space, the procedures for preparing the house and collecting and storing the equipment. In a family with young children, it is better for the conversation to summarize the preparations and be used mainly to present the protected space, the equipment, and the like.

Is it advisable to talk to children about emergencies?

Yes. Such a conversation gives them a sense of security. Mediating situations to children helps in connecting it to their world and provides an opportunity to create order, experiential organization and emotional processing. Adapted and professional emergency education, which includes preparation and practice, will help children cope optimally in real time and will also contribute to the development of personal and social resilience.

Why is it not recommended to allow children to watch the news during an emergency?

Children and teenagers are usually inundated with a lot of knowledge, most of which is not mediated and not adapted to their age. In light of this, they may develop concerns and fear that can increase and create an experience of helplessness.
Being exposed to the events does not have to be direct to affect the children's feelings or cause them distress. Therefore, even watching the news during emergency events can be a difficult experience for them.

Why is it important to maintain children’s daily routine?

An emergency is often accompanied by a heightened sense of uncertainty and a feeling of lack of control. A regular routine helps reduce these feelings and provides children with a sense of stability and security. To achieve this, it's essential to establish a family daily routine that includes, for example, specific times for activities such as waking up, family meals, activities, household chores, and bedtime.
