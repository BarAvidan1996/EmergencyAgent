How to deal with Stress and Anxiety in a War?

Colonel (ret.) Prof. Yaron Bar Dayan, an expert in internal medicine, with tips that will allow us to sleep better and function better, even in war time

In war time, almost all of us feel fear, sadness, lack of will to get out of bed and to be active in our lives. This is the body's natural reaction to traumatic events, which we will all experience to one extent or the other at time of war, as part of the severe change in routine we all experience. However, what is important following the experience – is how we deal with it, and how we help ourselves return to routine.


Colonel (ret.) Prof. Yaron Bar Dayan, an expert in internal medicine and a Home Front Command's public representative, answers the important questions. How many of us experience trauma in war time? Do we recover from it? How do we identify stress and fear and how do we deal with them? And how do we deal with grief?

 

Most of us will experience feelings of stress and fear in war time

In time of war, people who relate to their feelings, and according to Prof. Bar Dayan this does not apply to everyone – will feel sad, will be afraid, will wake up at night or not fall asleep. The reason is that close to 100% of us experience some sort of trauma during a war. However, it is a short-term phenomenon: approximately 505 of those experiencing trauma will overcome it within three days. Within a month, almost 95% of people will be back to functioning fully. The problem is that the 5% who will not overcome the trauma, are at risk of suffering from post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), and therefore, according to Prof. Bar Dayan, it is important to teach ourselves how to deal with these feelings.


First, to separate the traumatic experience from reality, it is very important to separate daytime and nighttime – between sleep and wakefulness. The main way to do so, is by adopting regular habits. Waking up, brushing teeth, and shaving. This is why in the army, for example, even in war time, there is a routine of wearing uniform, shaving and shining the shoes. It forces us to look after ourselves. "When a person performs tasks that separate day from night, he turns from a sleeping person to a waking person.", the internal medicine expert explains.


The second significant part relates to the end of the day. In order to be able to fall asleep, we must enter sleeping mode at a set time, more or less, and it must be separate from other actions we perform in the daytimes, like eating or exposing ourselves to screens. Additionally, we make sure we sleep over 7 hours a night, as it is known to extend life, to improve the quality of life and our health. The third thing the professor touches on is our dreams. In the three days following the trauma we cannot control our dreams and they may be difficult, but after three days we can influence the quality of sleep, and it is important to do so with the air of a pillow, a good mattress, a dark room and set times.

 

Four ways to maintain our emotional health in time of war

Prof. Bar Dayan suggests dividing the day into four parts, that will allow us to look after ourselves even in times of war and create the foundation for going back to our normal selves on the day after the war – nutrition, movement, activity and respite.

 

Nutrition

"In war time people can be divided into three types, in terms of their nutrition", Prof. Bar Dayan explains. "There are those who eat when stressed – the first type eats erratically, everything in sight. The second type does not make sure they eat or drink at all. Both groups put themselves at risk.". according to the doctor, order has an essential role in war time. In the same way it is important to get up in the morning and get ready for your day, it is important to eat. So, we need to prepare ourselves a plate of breakfast, which we don't have to finish. But as long as we keep a daily routine, with a set time for meals, we will not snack during the day, and if we make sure we drink water, we won't dehydrate. "This is crucial, because in terms of order, the body gets all what it needs and physically, if it not stressed. Because when you don’t eat – you are stressed. This is the main part. Emotionally, it is important because when I am full, the physiological sensation gives me emotional fullness. I am satisfied because I am not hungry, which affects the physical state. It is called satisfaction for a reason", he stresses.

 

Movement

Even in times of war, it is important to perform physical activity like running, swimming or power walking. However, in many places in Israel, this is currently not possible because of the defensive guidelines. So, how do we maintain an active lifestyle at times of war? "My parents walk 10,000 steps around the living room table, but this is not compulsory", Prof. Bar Dayan explains, what is important, he explains, if that movement impacts circulation, which is important because we need to crate aerobic activity that stimulates the body.


So, even when we find ourselves in safe spaces for an extended period, there is activity that you can perform, such as squats, moving from sitting to standing position, or strength exercises. Stretching is also important, as it maintains the flexibility and muscle lifespan. Finally, it is also important to maintain balance, by doing yoga or standing on one foot – things that can also be turned into games with the children that will help pass the times.

 

Activity

Social activity is important, as is work. Those who can work outside or at home, it is important, as works helps us overcome trauma in the fastest way possible. If I cannot go out, it is possible to volunteer – it is good for the soul. Helping others in war time is critical. If you have elderly or lone neighbors, it is important o assist them, to see if they are OK, to talk to them, to support them.

 

Respite

Quality respite is the time between the end of the day and sleep. It is a critical part of the day before going to sleep, in which we clear our head and prepare ourselves for sleep. Prof. Bar Dayan suggests two main ways of preparing ourselves.


Breathing: simply breathe 10 or 12 deep breaths, give ourselves time, disconnect from the world, breathe through the nose slowly, and clear the mind from thoughts of the day.


Visualization of meditation: lie down and think of good things that happened or that we want to happen. With 5 minutes of breaths, meditation, or visualization we will fall asleep quicker, sleep better and will enjoy our lives more. This is how we return life to their path in these difficult times – from reaction we move on to action.

 

How do you deal with loss in a war?

It is hard to imagine how a person who lost a loved one in the war, can think of stopping at the end of each day to practice breathing before going to bed, without thinking. According to Prof. Bar Dayan, it is almost impossible. However, he claims that Judaism provides a very good explanation to the period of mourning. "In Judaism we have the Shiva, then a month, then 11 months and then a year. It is in line with our stages of dealing with loss", he explains.


At first, it is natural that the loss is unbearable, to us and to those around us. Gradually, in time, we bring ourselves back to normal life, to routine. It will not happen quickly, it will take time, but in most cases, after a year we will be at a level of functioning in which the grief will still be with us – but it will not define how we function all day, every day. When you lose a loved one, it is like losing part of ourselves, but gradually, you remember the smile before the tears come.

Q&A

How can I help a person having an anxiety attack near me?

MDA recommend the following steps to provide first aid to people having an anxiety attack:

• Acting and doing – it is a good idea to encourage the person affected to be active, for example, ask them to stand up, or suggest that they walk.
• Deep breaths – suggest they take deep breaths to relax the body. Do that by talking deeps breaths for 5 seconds. You can use relaxation techniques such as meditation, yoga and guided imagery.
• Emphasize the coping ability – it is important to explain the person that their feelings are legitimate in the situation, that the event is indeed threatening, and as such it invokes difficult reactions. Strengthen them by saying that thy can overcome this event too, despite the difficulty.
• Information – it is a good idea to provide details of the event, to tell them that the attack has ended and that the immediate danger has passed. It is important to repeat this message.
• Getting help from family members – find our if there are relatives and friends which the person can contact and ask to stay with them.
• Distraction – Talk to the person about other topics to focus them on encouraging thoughts.
• Less touch – often people try to hold out a hand to panic attack victims and to support their bodies so they don't fall, but these actions can have the opposite effect and increase the sense of helplessness and personal inability felt by the person. Respect their personal space and ask what you can do to help them.

How can I recognize an anxiety attack?

An anxiety attack is caused by emotional stress, for example, because of exposure to rocket fire. This is a natural reaction caused by the hormones overwhelming the body as a result of the threat, that increase fear, uncertainty and even helplessness, even if for a short time.

You can recognize an anxiety attack as it will include increased heart rate, elevated blood pressure, sweating, tremors, a feeling of being unable to move, faintness or loss of consciousness. Additionally, emotions such as overwhelming fear, nervousness, inability to connect with the surroundings, anger, desperation and lack of control will increase.

How can helping others improve my ability to cope with feelings of stress and anxiety in the war?

Helping others around us creates a sense of usefulness and value, and helps to overcome feelings of stress and uncertainty, which is very useful for those who need that (lonely, elderly ill people, etc.). It is very important to create collaboration and mutual helps between neighbors, so it is a good idea to have a conversation about getting organized together, in addition to even family getting organized at home. This link explains how to help those around us to cope with feelings of stress an anxiety in times of emergency.

The war could go on for a long time. How can I reduce the levels of stress and anxiety?

First, in times of emergency, it is important to look after the family's physical needs – food and water, rest and sleep. In addition, we will identify what helped us in the past when we had a problem or stress and use that. It is also possible to increase the sense of control of the situation by creating a set routine. I feel stress and anxiety, but I am afraid of sharing them with my family and appear weak, what can I do?

The ability to express emotions is a sign of strength, not weakness. It is important to have a family conversation to strengthen support and sense of "togetherness", to express the different thoughts, feelings and reactions of family members during this time, to show that they are normal and shared by many people and to create a feeling that we can go on. In case of extreme emotional distress, you can call the ERAN helpline on 1201 extension 6 or Enosh (a helpline for people dealing with emotional issues and their families) on 075-7556155.
